<intro>The EB-5 capital stack is the layered financing structure of a project — senior debt, mezzanine debt, preferred equity, and common equity — and it determines where your $800,000 investment sits in the repayment queue if the project underperforms. EB-5 funds should occupy a subordinate position behind bank debt and developer equity, not be the primary or sole source of project financing. A project where EB-5 capital makes up more than half of total project financing carries materially higher repayment risk. The sections below explain each layer, how intercreditor agreements protect (or fail to protect) EB-5 investors, and the capital stack red flags to screen during due diligence.</intro>

## Capital Stack 101

It is rare for a single entity to provide all the necessary funding in any commercial real estate venture. Instead, developers assemble capital from various investors and sources with each subsection assigned different terms, risk levels and potential returns. The capital stack is a visual representation of how these sources are layered to finance a real estate project. Think of it as a hierarchy of capital with each position determining the order in which the investor gets paid back. The capital stack is broadly divided into two main categories: debt and equity. Within these categories there are four primary layers. Common equity is the highest level of risk and offers the highest return. Common equity is the first to absorb losses and the last to be repaid but has the greatest potential for high returns. Preferred equity is paid out before common equity but after all debt and often receives a fixed rate of return. Mezzanine debt is subordinate to senior debt but senior to all equity positions. Mezzanine debt is often secured by a pledge of the borrower's equity in the project. Senior debt is typically from a bank and has the first priority for treatment and is secured by the project itself.

## Senior Debt

Senior debt is the most secure and the lowest cost form of financing in the capital stack. Senior debt is typically provided by traditional lenders like banks and is essentially a mortgage on the property itself. In the event of a default, the senior debt holder has the first claim on the property and can foreclose on it to recover their investment.

## Mezzanine Debt

Mezzanine debt bridges the gap between senior debt and equity. It is a more expensive form of financing than senior debt due to its higher risk. Mezzanine loans are subordinate to senior debt which means the mezzanine lender only gets paid after the senior lender has been repaid. Mezzanine debt is typically secured by a pledge of the ownership interest in the entity that owns the property.

## Preferred Equity

Preferred Equity is a hybrid between debt and equity. It is an investment in the project, but it comes with a predetermined fixed rate of return that must be paid before common equity holders receive any distributions. While preferred equity holders have priority over common equity holders they are still subordinate to all debt holders including senior and mezzanine debt.

## Common Equity

Common equity is the highest return layer with the highest risk profile. Common equity is typically contributed by the project developer and other investors who are willing to take on the most risk. These investors are the last to get paid and they only receive a return on their investment after all debt and preferred equity obligations have been met. However, they also have the potential for the greatest reward. If the project is successful common equity holders are entitled to all remaining profits which in some cases can be substantial.

## Where Does EB-5 Capital Fit in the Stack?

EB-5 capital must remain at risk for the duration of the investment so EB-5 cannot be structured as senior debt which typically has strong security and repayment guarantees. EB-5 funds are most commonly positioned in the middle of the capital stack as either mezzanine debt or preferred equity.

This positioning allows developers to fill the funding gap between the senior loan and their own equity contribution. For the EB-5 investor, this position carries more risk than senior debt but is more secure than common equity. The specific choice between mezzanine debt or preferred equity structure can have significant implications for the investor's right and remedies in the event of a project's failure. Understanding where EB-5 capital fits within the capital stack prior to investment is extremely important because it influences repayment priority and overall risk exposure.

## What Are Other Key Considerations for EB-5 Investors?

A significant equity contribution from the project developer is one of the most important indicators for potential investors. Skin in the game is a good indicator of the developer's commitment to the project's success. A developer who has a substantial amount of their own capital at risk is more likely to be motivated to see the project's completion and ensure its profitability. Investors should be wary of projects where the developer has little of their own money invested as this could suggest a lack of confidence in the project or a willingness to take on excessive risk with other people's money.

### Senior Debt Terms

The terms of the senior loan can have a significant impact on the overall risk profile of the project even though senior loans are not EB-5 capital. A high loan to cost or loan to value ratio on the senior debt may indicate that the project is overleveraged. If the project's income is not sufficient to cover the senior debt payments, it could lead to default and foreclosure which would ultimately wipe out the subordinate layers of the capital stack which includes the EB-5 investment.

### EB-5 Repayment Position and Intercreditor Agreements

Investors should carefully examine the exact position of their investment within the capital stack. Is the EB-5 capital the only layer of mezzanine debt or preferred equity, or are there other similar layers? If so, what is the priority of repayment among them? These details are often spelled out in an intercreditor agreement (a legal document that governs the rights and priorities of different lenders in a financing). This agreement is crucial as it dictates the rules of engagement in a default scenario which includes standstill provision (where a junior lender agrees not to take action against the borrower for a certain period) and rights to cure defaults in the senior loan.

## What Are Common Risks for EB-5 Projects and Risk Mitigation Strategies?

### Common Risk Factors

**Over-Leveraging:** A project that is too heavily reliant on debt is more vulnerable to financial instability which could jeopardize the repayment of the investment and the creation of the required jobs.

**Weak Exit Strategy:** The project's business plan must have a clear and credible exit strategy for repaying the EB-5 investors. This could be a sale of the property, a refinancing of the debt, or profits from ongoing operations. A vague or unrealistic exit strategy is a major red flag.

**Market Fluctuations:** Economic downturns can have a significant impact on real estate projects and affect everything from constructions costs and timelines to rental income and property values.

### How to Mitigate Risk

**Thorough Due Diligence:** Investors should conduct their own due diligence on the project, the developer and the RC. This includes interviewing the developers, analyzing their track record, and reviewing the project's financials and the local market conditions.

**Independent Professional Advice:** It is recommended that investors work with experienced immigration attorneys, immigration brokers and other professionals who can help them evaluate the investment and the capital stack structure.

**Focus on Quality Projects:** Look for projects with strong fundamentals, a conservative capital structure and a reputable developer with a lengthy track record of success.

## A Strong Capital Stack is the Foundation of a Strong EB-5 Investment

The capital stack is more than just a financial infographic. The capital stack is a roadmap that outlines the risk and reward for every party involved in a real estate project. For EB-5 investors, a thorough understanding of the capital stack is non-negotiable.

By carefully evaluating the layers of financing, the developers' commitment, terms of the senior debt and their own position within the stack, investors can make more informed decisions. A well-structured capital stack, with a balanced approach to debt and equity provides a strong foundation for a successful project that can meet both the financial and immigration objectives of the EB-5 investor.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the capital stack in an EB-5 investment and why is it important?

The capital stack refers to the structured financial hierarchy of the different financing sources that make up an investment. The financing sources are primarily divided into debt and equity layers such as common equity, preferred equity, <a href="/research/eb5-capital-stack">mezzanine debt and senior debt</a>. Understanding the capital stack is crucial because it directly impacts investors risk exposure, repayment priority and the overall stability of the project. It provides a roadmap for assessing how funds are allocated and where EB-5 capital fits within the financing structure.

### Where does EB-5 capital typically fit within the capital stack and what does "at risk" mean?

EB-5 capital must remain "at risk" throughout the sustainment period meaning it cannot be positioned as senior debt. EB-5 funds are typically placed in the middle layers of the capital stack as either mezz debt or preferred equity. This placement carries more risk than senior debt but is more secure than common equity. EB-5 capital must remain at risk throughout the sustainment period meaning the investment is subject to potential loss and not guaranteed repayment. This stipulation aligns with <a href="/research/complete-2027-eb5-guide">EB-5 program requirements</a> for demonstrating investment risk.

### What key factors should EB-5 investors consider regarding senior debt and developer contributions?

Investors should carefully evaluate the senior debt terms, including loan to cost and loan to value ratios because high leverage in a project can signal financial vulnerability and risk of default which would jeopardize subordinate capital like EB-5 investment capital. Additionally, investors should look for significant equity contribution from the project developer because it demonstrates the developer's commitment to the project (AKA skin in the game). Projects where the developer has minimal personal investment should always warrant caution. For a comprehensive checklist, see our guide on <a href="/research/19-questions-rural-eb5-project-due-diligence">19 questions every EB-5 investor should ask</a>.

### How can EB-5 investors mitigate common risks associated with the capital stack and project investment?

To mitigate risks such as overleveraging, weak exit strategies and market fluctuations investors should conduct thorough due diligence on the project, developer and RC which includes reviewing financials and interviewing developers. Seeking independent professional advice from experienced immigration attorneys and brokers is recommended. Finally, focusing on quality projects with conservative capital structures and reputable developers enhances the likelihood of successful investment outcomes.
