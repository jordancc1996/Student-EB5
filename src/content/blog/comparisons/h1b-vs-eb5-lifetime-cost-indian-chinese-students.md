<intro>The financial calculus for international students and their families weighing U.S. schools usually focuses on the immediate costs of education and the immigration process. Those costs are heavy, but they are dwarfed by a less visible one. An analysis of lifetime earnings reveals that for most international students, the true cost of returning home after graduation is measured in millions of dollars. The EB-5 immigrant investor program offers a direct route to permanent residency that secures long-term financial benefits while bypassing the structural flaws of the H-1B system.</intro>

## What Is the Lifetime Earnings Gap?

Data from the Economic Innovation Group shows that workers who first arrived in the United States on student visas and remained permanently in the country earned significantly more than their native-born counterparts. In 2023, these workers earned a median salary of $115,000 compared to $87,000 for the median native-born worker with at least a college degree. For Indian nationals specifically, the median income is $146,000 annually, more than two-thirds higher than the median income of an American-born college graduate.

The disparity becomes even more obvious when comparing the earnings of international students who stay in the United States to those who return to their home countries. A software engineer in the United States earns an average of $110,000 to $150,000 annually. The same role in India typically pays between $7,000 and $14,000. Even in higher-compensation regions like the UAE, software engineers earn between $43,000 and $100,000 annually.

Over a 40-year career, this annual gap compounds into a massive discrepancy in lifetime wealth. The Georgetown University Center on Education and the Workforce estimates lifetime earnings for a U.S. bachelor's degree holder at $2.8M, and $3.8M for engineering and STEM graduates. If an international student earns $120,000 mid-career in the United States while a similarly qualified counterpart earns $30,000 to $40,000 in their home country, the lifetime earnings gap approaches $3M to $4M. This calculation does not even account for wealth accrued through investments, retirement accounts, and real estate over a lifetime.

## Flaws of the H-1B System

The H-1B visa carries significant limitations and inherent instability for international workers. It is a nonimmigrant visa tied to a specific employer, which naturally suppresses wages and limits job mobility. This dependency diminishes the worker's bargaining power in salary negotiations. Research by the National Bureau of Economic Research indicates that H-1B workers earn approximately 16% less than their American counterparts for the same job.

The annual statutory cap of 85,000 visas has produced a lottery system where demand far outstrips supply. According to the American Association of Universities, roughly one in four STEM graduates on temporary visas does not manage to stay in the United States. The H-1B program continues to become more difficult, with policy changes targeting the program directly. The new $100,000 fee on most new H-1B petitions and a shift to a wage-weighted selection model that favors mid-career hires over fresh graduates have already stacked the odds against recent international alumni.

For those who do secure an H-1B visa, the path to permanent residency via the EB-2 and EB-3 categories is characterized by substantial backlogs. During this prolonged waiting period (which can extend for over a decade), the H-1B worker remains in temporary status and is subject to a 60-day grace period to find new employment if laid off. ## The Case for High-Skilled Immigration

The economic benefits of high-skilled immigration extend far beyond the individual worker. Research indicates that expanding high-skilled immigration achieves three critical goals simultaneously: it boosts the rate of economic growth, reduces inequality, and saves taxpayers significant sums of money.

Immigrants are highly entrepreneurial. 55% of U.S. startup companies valued at $1 billion or more were founded by immigrants. They also author or co-author 30% of patents within U.S. industries critical for economic and national security.

## The EB-5 Advantage and the 2026 Grandfathering Deadline

The EB-5 immigrant investor program provides a direct and predictable path to a green card and circumvents both the H-1B lottery and the employment-based backlogs. The program requires a standard investment of $800,000 in a Targeted Employment Area or $1,050,000 in a non-targeted area. Most importantly, EB-5 is self-sponsored and removes the employer dependence from the immigrant investor equation.

The EB-5 Reform and Integrity Act of 2022 introduced concurrent filing, which allows U.S.-based applicants to file their Adjustment of Status (Form I-485) alongside their EB-5 petition (Form I-526E). This permits the applicant and their dependent family members to remain in the United States with unrestricted work authorization and travel privileges while the petition is adjudicated.

The Reform and Integrity Act included a critical <a href="/research/eb5-grandfathering-2026-2027-deadlines">grandfathering provision</a> that protects investors who file their I-526E petitions on or before September 30, 2026. This provision ensures that petitions filed by this date will continue to be processed by USCIS even if the regional center program is not reauthorized beyond its current expiration date. Filing after this date exposes investors to regulatory and political risk, as well as expected increases in the minimum investment amount to account for inflation. Many industry professionals expect an increase to $920,000–$940,000 for TEA projects and $1.1M–$1.2M for non-TEA projects.

## Closing Thoughts

For families evaluating the $800,000 investment, the return is not solely the yield on the capital deployed. The true return is the millions of dollars in additional lifetime earnings secured by allowing a child to build their career in the United States. Green card holders are also eligible for federal aid, including Pell Grants and federally subsidized loans, which are unavailable to international students on visas. The return on a properly structured EB-5 investment pales in comparison to the initial capital outlay, provided the family chooses the right EB-5 project.
