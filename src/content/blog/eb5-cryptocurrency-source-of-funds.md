<intro>The EB-5 Immigrant Investor Program requires foreign nationals to invest a minimum of $800,000 in a targeted employment area or $1,050,000 in a non-targeted employment area project. A core requirement of the program is that the EB-5 investor must prove the investment capital comes from a lawful source. The crypto market may be in a downturn, but digital assets have become extremely popular as wealth creation investments in recent years. Many prospective EB-5 investors are interested in using cryptocurrency as a source of investment capital. We hope this article provides clarity on the requirements, challenges, and viability of using crypto assets to fund an EB-5 investment.</intro>

## USCIS Position on Cryptocurrency

The United States Citizenship and Immigration Services does not explicitly prohibit the use of cryptocurrency for EB-5 investments, but it does not offer blanket approvals either. USCIS officials have stated that the agency evaluates virtual currencies on a case-by-case basis.

<blockquote class="my-8 border-l-4 border-foreground pl-6 italic text-lg font-serif">"USCIS cannot provide blanket assurances regarding any particular form of transfer, but we will continue to evaluate evidence provided by petitioners to determine whether the relevant statutory and regulatory requirements have been met, including evidence that the funds invested belong to the petitioner and were acquired directly and indirectly and by lawful means."<footer class="not-italic text-sm mt-3 text-muted-foreground">— U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services, Source of Funds Guidance</footer></blockquote>

USCIS requires the same standard of proof for cryptocurrency as it does for fiat currency. The petitioner must establish that the digital assets were acquired lawfully and belong to the investor.

## Source of Funds and Path of Funds Documentation

Proving the source of funds and path of funds is the most demanding part of an EB-5 petition involving cryptocurrency. USCIS has increased its scrutiny of digital asset transactions, which has resulted in more requests for evidence in recent years. ### Establishing Wallet Ownership and Identity

Digital wallets are not automatically linked to an individual's legal identity, unlike traditional bank accounts. To satisfy USCIS, the investor must prove they own the digital wallet used in the transaction. This can be done by:

- Providing account profiles from cryptocurrency exchanges that connect the investor's name, email, physical address, and photograph to the wallet.
- Showing the transaction history of the wallet to demonstrate control.
- Performing specific cryptographic verifications or signed-message proofs to demonstrate ownership of the private keys.

### Active Trading vs. Long-Term Holding

The ease of documenting cryptocurrency depends on how the investor managed the assets. Long-term holders who purchased cryptocurrency years ago and held it in a single wallet have a simpler documentation path. For example, if an investor purchased Bitcoin five years ago on a regulated exchange and never moved it, they only need to show:

- The initial purchase transaction
- The source of the fiat funds used for the purchase
- The bank records from that period

Active traders who move cryptocurrency across multiple exchanges and wallets face a higher burden of proof. They must provide transaction ledgers from every exchange used and show a clear, unbroken chain of trades. This is similar to providing brokerage statements for active stock trading.

### Mining Cryptocurrency

For investors who obtain their cryptocurrency through mining, documentation is a little different. Miners must show receipts for the purchase of specialized mining hardware and software, proof that the funds used to purchase the mining equipment were legally earned, ongoing operational costs (primarily electricity bills, which are the largest expense in mining), and evidence that the mined cryptocurrency was deposited directly from the mining pool into the investor's designated wallet.

## Structuring the Transaction and Path of Funds

An EB-5 investment cannot be made directly in cryptocurrency because regional centers and escrow banks require U.S. dollars. The cryptocurrency must be converted into fiat currency before it is wired to the project.

### Conversion to U.S. Dollars

The investor must liquidate the cryptocurrency through a licensed exchange or payment processor. The financial institution performing the conversion is responsible for conducting know-your-customer and anti-money-laundering checks. After conversion, the U.S. dollars are deposited into the investor's personal bank account and then wired to the EB-5 project's escrow account. By doing this, the investor creates a clear path of funds that is easily traceable for USCIS.

### Parallel Transfers and Currency Restrictions

Investors from countries with strict capital controls, such as China, Vietnam, and Bangladesh, sometimes use cryptocurrency to bypass domestic bank transfer limits. Historically, investors used "third-party parallel transfers," where they transferred local currency to a third party who then transferred U.S. currency from an offshore account to the investor. USCIS now requires these third parties to document the lawful source of their own funds, which makes this method difficult, if not impossible.

Cryptocurrency has become an alternative to this routing mechanism. Even though cryptocurrency mining and holding are not illegal in China, Vietnam, or Bangladesh, using digital assets to evade state capital controls can attract scrutiny from USCIS. The agency checks whether the transaction violates local laws, as an investor must comply with both U.S. immigration regulations and the laws of their home country. ## Tax and Regulatory Implications

In the United States, different federal agencies define cryptocurrency differently. The IRS treats cryptocurrency as property, and when an investor sells crypto to fund their EB-5 investment, they trigger a taxable event. The investor must pay capital gains tax if the asset has appreciated since its purchase. USCIS will look for tax returns showing that these capital gains were paid. The Commodity Futures Trading Commission treats Bitcoin as a commodity, and the Securities and Exchange Commission treats certain digital assets, such as security tokens, as securities, though Bitcoin is classified as a commodity. These definitions do not conflict, but they require investors to ensure their transactions comply with all applicable tax and securities laws.

## Funding Your EB-5 with Crypto

Cryptocurrency is an acceptable source of funds for an EB-5 investment as long as the investor can document the entire life cycle of the asset. The success of the petition relies on:

- Proving the legal origin of the initial capital used to buy or mine the cryptocurrency
- Verifying wallet ownership
- Maintaining an unbroken transaction ledger through the liquidation process
