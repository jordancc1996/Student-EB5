<intro>The EB-5 process is convoluted and complex for new investors who want to use the program to secure permanent residency in the United States. The EB-5 program grants a green card to investors who place qualifying capital into a U.S. commercial enterprise that creates at least 10 U.S. jobs. This Part 1 guide focuses on the pre-investment phase: the eligibility, financial, and project-selection decisions Indian and Chinese F-1 students and H-1B professionals must work through before they file Form I-526E.</intro>

## Part 1: Starting the EB-5 Journey

The EB-5 program is a straightforward proposition. Make a qualifying investment that creates jobs for U.S. workers, and in return the United States grants you permanent residency. A single EB-5 petition (Form I-526E for regional center investors, or Form I-526 for direct investors) covers the entire immediate family, including the primary investor, the spouse, and any unmarried children under the age of 21 at the time of filing.

Most investors choose a project located in a Targeted Employment Area (TEA), where the minimum investment is $800,000, instead of the $1,050,000 required for projects outside a TEA. After the project is completed and the required jobs are created, the investor typically receives the original capital back in 3 to 7 years, with negligible interest. In structural terms, the investor is providing a low-cost loan to a U.S. real estate developer in exchange for a green card for the family.

## Two Critical Pre-Investment Checks

Before you sign a subscription agreement or wire any capital, two questions decide whether you can move forward at all: are you an accredited investor, and can you fully document the lawful source of your $800,000.

### A. Investor Eligibility: The Accredited Investor Test

To invest in an EB-5 regional center project, you must qualify as an "accredited investor" under U.S. securities law. EB-5 investments are private securities offerings, so this rule exists to ensure you have the financial sophistication to absorb the economic risk of a private real estate investment.

There are two standard ways to meet the threshold:

- **Income Test:** The primary applicant has an annual income of at least $200,000 in each of the last two years (or $300,000 jointly with a spouse), with a reasonable expectation of the same in the current year.
- **Net Worth Test:** The applicant has a net worth of at least $1,000,000. The value of the primary residence is excluded from this calculation, but assets held jointly with a spouse may be included.

If you do not meet either test outright, do not panic. Experienced immigration and securities counsel can structure the investment, for example through a qualifying co-investor, gift, or restructured asset base, to satisfy the standard while remaining compliant with USCIS rules.

### B. Source of Funds: Where Most Investors Get Stuck

Source of funds is the most complex part of the EB-5 application and the area where most petitions are delayed or denied. You need $800,000 in cash that can be wired to the new commercial enterprise, and you must demonstrate the legality of every dollar to USCIS. The standard is a complete, unbroken chain of evidence showing the funds were obtained lawfully.

Acceptable sources include:

- Salaried income and bonuses (especially relevant for H-1B professionals)
- Stock and equity sales, including RSUs and ESPP proceeds
- Cash savings accumulated over multiple years
- Cryptocurrency liquidations with full exchange records
- 401(k) and IRA distributions (subject to U.S. tax and penalty rules)
- Secured and unsecured loans against documented collateral
- Financial gifts from parents or close family members

Each source requires its own evidentiary package: years of tax returns, bank statements, brokerage statements, property records, employment letters, and, for gifts, the giver's own source-of-funds trail. The burden of proof is entirely on the investor to show that not a single dollar of the $800,000 came from illicit activities.

Indian and Chinese investors face additional layers: Indian investors must navigate the Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS) $250,000 per-person annual cap, often pooling family remittances across two or three taxpayers. Chinese investors must work around the $50,000 SAFE annual foreign exchange quota, typically using multiple family members to legally convert and transfer capital. Both jurisdictions require careful coordination with cross-border tax counsel.

### StudentEB5 Recommendation: Avoid Loans From the Regional Center

It may seem convenient to borrow money from the entity sponsoring your investment, but USCIS treats loans originating from the regional center as a structural conflict of interest. USCIS has repeatedly taken the position that such loans do not satisfy the "at risk" requirement, which can jeopardize the petition and lead to denial. Capital should come from sources independent of the regional center, the new commercial enterprise, and the job-creating entity.

## Regional Center and Project Selection

Selecting the right regional center and the right project is the most consequential decision you will make in the EB-5 process. The project you choose dictates both the safety of your invested capital and the likelihood of immigration success. The rise of bespoke regional centers competing with the major sponsors has shifted the burden of due diligence squarely onto the investor.

The practical advice is simple: interview multiple regional centers, and only work with those that have a documented track record of I-526E approvals and successful capital repayment to prior investors. Compare project offerings on both immigration risk (job creation methodology, I-956F approval, processing category) and financial risk (capital stack, developer experience, repayment structure). EB-5 investor communities on Facebook and LinkedIn are useful for hearing firsthand experiences from investors who have already vetted specific sponsors.

To narrow the field in a crowded market, prioritize projects with the following attributes:

- **Rural TEA Designation:** Projects located in designated rural areas qualify for priority processing from USCIS. Rural petitions move ahead of urban and high-unemployment petitions in the adjudication queue, which materially reduces wait time. For Indian and Chinese investors, who face the most severe visa backlogs in other employment-based categories, a rural project is effectively mandatory to avoid waiting years or decades for a visa number.
- **I-956F Approval:** The project must have an approved or filed Form I-956F. This document confirms that USCIS has reviewed the project's business plan, economic analysis, and job creation methodology. An approved I-956F removes a major layer of adjudication risk from your individual I-526E.
- **Healthy Capital Stack:** Look for meaningful developer equity and a balanced senior loan position. The developer should have substantial "skin in the game" alongside the EB-5 capital. A project that is overly dependent on EB-5 funding is a major red flag because it signals that traditional lenders and equity investors declined to take the same risk.
- **Excellent Developer Track Record:** The real estate developer should have a clean record of completed projects, no bankruptcies within the last twenty to thirty years, and verifiable references from prior EB-5 investors.
- **Repayment Structure:** Look for a structural repayment guarantee between the New Commercial Enterprise (NCE, the entity pooling the EB-5 funds) and the Job Creating Entity (JCE, the developer). The guarantee cannot run directly to the investor, because that would conflict with the strict USCIS requirement that your capital remain "at risk," but it can be structured at the NCE-to-JCE level.

## What to Expect Next

Once you have confirmed accredited investor status, organized your source-of-funds documentation with counsel, and selected a vetted rural TEA project with an approved I-956F, you are ready to move into the filing phase: subscription documents, capital wire, and Form I-526E (with concurrent I-485, I-765, and I-131 if you are already in the United States on a valid H-1B or F-1 status).
